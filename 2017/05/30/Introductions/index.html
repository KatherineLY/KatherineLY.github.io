2. Electronic Portfolio in Education

The electronic portfolio in education is a collection of a students’work that can advance learning by providing a way for them to organize, archive, and display work. The electronic format allows an instructor to evaluate student portfolios via the Internet, CD-ROM, DVD, or zip disk.

Electronic portfolios have become a popular alternative to paper-based portfolios because they provide the opportunity to review, communicate and give feedback in an asynchronous manner. In addition, students are able to reflect on their work, which makes the experience of creating the e-portfolio meaningful.

Six major functions in education e-portfolios:

  • Document skills and learning
  • Record and track development within a program
  • Plan educational programs
  • Evaluate and monitor performance
  • Evaluate a course
  • Find a job

In general e-portfolios promote critical thinking and support the development of technology literacy skills.

3. Language Learning Theory

  • Chapter 1. Language learning in early childhood
    In chapter1, we learned about some research on children’s early language development and three theoretical perspectives for explaining first language acquisition.
    In general, there’s developmental sequences in children’s early language development and the order of acquisition vary among different children, which related to children’s congnitive development and metalinguistic awareness development. However, some similarities were found in the acquisition of negation and questions, and there are detail stages in the acquisition of the two aspects. Before the school age, children develop the ability to use language to understand others and to express their own meanings, and after they going to school, they develop their metalinguistic awareness and vocabulary through reading. And three theoretical perspectives are the behaviourist perspective, the innatist perspective and interactionist/ developmental perspectives. And some researches about children’s language learning in bilingual environment and language disorders and delays are also discussed.
  • Chapter 2. Second language learning
    In Chapter2, we focused on some of the findings of research that examine the language development of second language learners. Like the language learning in early childhood, second language learners experience developmental sequences too. Moreover, they have to overcome the influence from their first language. And in order to analyze learners’ speech simply as an incorrect version of the target language, there are contrastive analysis, error analysis and interlanguage. And we also discussed about communicative competence in this Chapter including vocabulary, pragmatics and pronunciation.
  • Chapter 3. Individual differences in second language learning
    In Chapter3, we turn our attantion to some of the ways in which learners differ from each other and how those individual differencess affect how quickly and how well they succeed in second language. The main points of the individual differences are intelligence, language learning aptitude, learning styles, personality,motivation in the classroom, indentity and ethnic group affiliation and learner beliefs. In addition, relationships between individual characteristics and learning environments are complex, different learners will react differently to the same learning conditions, and even the same learner will react differently to the same conditions at different times. And the complicated relationships and factors above affect learners’ language learning success.
  • Chapter 4. Explaining second language learning
    Just like the title said, in Chapter4, we learned second language learning more deeply by explaining the behaviourist perspective, the innatist perspective, the cognitive perdpective and sociocultural perspectives. All the theories of language acquistion are intended to account for is the ability of learners to acquire language within a variety of social and instructional environment.
